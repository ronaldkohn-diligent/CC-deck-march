Connected Compliance — March 2026
"The first thing I do when I join a new organisation is spend months doing the hard work of pulling everything together — so that if I've been out for two weeks, I can sit down and immediately understand what is going on across my entire compliance programme."
Adam Hunt — ex-Head of Global Ethics & Compliance, Netflix · Current Head of Risk & Compliance, Lime
Today, that job means logging into every tool in your stack — manually correlating data that was never designed to talk to each other. We're building the system that does it automatically, and gets smarter the more you use it.
What We're Building
An intelligence layer.
Not another product.
Connected Compliance sits horizontally across your compliance tools. It ingests signals from each, finds the correlations a human would miss, and surfaces what matters — in priority order, with a clear path to action. Its value compounds the more products you connect.
Policy Manager
Policies, attestation rates, review cycles, and regulatory alignment. What the rules are and whether people know them.
Speak Up / Vault
Case volumes, regional patterns, investigation timelines. What is actually being reported — and what isn't.
Compliance Training
Completion rates, engagement gaps, knowledge blind spots. Whether people are actually prepared for the risks they face.
⟶
Built to extend. The same intelligence layer can plug into any Diligent product — risk assessments, governance workflows, third-party data, board reporting. E&C is the starting point. The architecture has no ceiling.

Design Response
What we heard. What we did.
Feedback from the ELT session — reflected in the design direction.
GRC Command Center — shown at ELT session, March 2026
"Cognitive load is very high. Too much going on."
Stripped back to a prioritised card feed. One item, one context, one action at a time.
"Why not just send a notification — like a colleague reaching out?"
Notifications handle push. Command Center handles pull and initiation. You need both.
"There are casual users who want a nudge, and power users who want it always on."
Lean-back and lean-in views. The system nudges you toward the power experience based on your behaviour.
"Condition them into a place — that's where they'll make requests too."
Contextual chat lives inside each card — scoped to the task, not a global assistant.
Design Principles
Design principles we're focused on.
Three principles that guide every screen — and shape how we think about the role of AI in the experience.
01
Tap, don't type.
The interface should answer most questions before anyone needs to write anything. Suggested actions, pre-loaded context, and structured inputs do the heavy lifting.
Chat is available for those who want it — but nobody should ever need it if the design is doing its job.
02
Review, don't create.
The agent completes the work. The user validates and acts. Nothing is auto-published — every output is a draft waiting for a human decision.
This is what makes it trustworthy to lawyers and regulators. The human is always in the loop, always the named decision-maker.
03
The system learns you.
Every action — a dismissal, a snooze, a correction — feeds back into how the system ranks what surfaces next. Over time it mirrors your actual priorities.
You're teaching it without doing any extra work. The more you use it, the less noise you see.
